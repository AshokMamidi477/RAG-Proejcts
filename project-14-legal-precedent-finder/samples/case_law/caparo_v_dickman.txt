CASE: Caparo Industries plc v Dickman [1990] 2 AC 605

JURISDICTION: House of Lords (UK)
AREA OF LAW: Tort — Negligence — Duty of Care — Economic Loss — Auditors

FACTS:
Caparo acquired shares in Fidelity plc relying on audited accounts prepared by Dickman (auditors).
The accounts were negligently prepared and significantly overstated the company's value.
Caparo suffered substantial financial loss and sued the auditors in negligence.

HELD:
The House of Lords held that the auditors did NOT owe a duty of care to Caparo as investors.
The auditors' duty was to the company and its existing shareholders as a body — not to individual
investors or potential investors making investment decisions.

THREE-STAGE TEST FOR DUTY OF CARE (Caparo Test):
Lord Bridge articulated a three-stage test that must ALL be satisfied:
1. Foreseeability: Was it foreseeable that the claimant might be harmed?
2. Proximity: Was there a sufficiently close relationship between the parties?
3. Fair, just and reasonable: Is it fair, just and reasonable to impose a duty?

APPLICATION:
This three-stage test refined the Donoghue neighbour principle and is now the primary test
for novel duty of care situations in English law. Courts approach incrementally by analogy
with established categories rather than applying a single general principle.

SIGNIFICANCE:
Essential case for any negligence analysis involving pure economic loss or professional services.
