CASE: Donoghue v Stevenson [1932] AC 562

JURISDICTION: House of Lords (UK)
AREA OF LAW: Tort — Negligence — Duty of Care

FACTS:
Mrs Donoghue consumed ginger beer from an opaque bottle purchased by a friend at a cafe.
The bottle contained a decomposed snail. She suffered gastroenteritis and nervous shock.
She could not sue in contract (the friend made the purchase). She sued the manufacturer in tort.

HELD:
The House of Lords (3-2) held that the manufacturer owed a duty of care to the ultimate consumer.
Lord Atkin established the 'neighbour principle': you must take reasonable care to avoid acts or
omissions which you can reasonably foresee would be likely to injure your neighbour.

'Neighbours' are persons so closely and directly affected by your act that you ought reasonably
to have them in contemplation when directing your mind to the acts or omissions in question.

LEGAL PRINCIPLE:
Established the modern law of negligence in the UK. For a duty of care to exist:
1. The defendant must be able to foresee that their conduct could cause harm to the claimant
2. There must be sufficient proximity between the parties
3. It must be fair, just and reasonable to impose a duty (later additions from Caparo)

SIGNIFICANCE:
Foundational case for tort law throughout common law jurisdictions. Overruled Winterbottom v Wright
and established that liability in negligence does not depend on contractual privity.
